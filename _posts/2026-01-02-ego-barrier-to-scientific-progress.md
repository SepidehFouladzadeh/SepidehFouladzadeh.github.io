Teamwork is endlessly praised in academia, framed as a virtue and a signal of excellence. Yet in practice, the higher we climb in education and expertise, the worse true collaboration often becomes. Our deep investment in one framework, one discipline, or one way of thinking quietly reduces our tolerance for other people’s rigor and makes us intellectually rigid. Too often, we hear, “this is a computational people’s problem”, “this is a theory people’s problem”, “this is an application problem”, and so on.

To me, progress today no longer comes from narrowing endlessly into depth, but rather from connecting concepts, staying curious, and being willing to say, “my perspective and my expertise are not sufficient on their own”. 

It seems to be the era where interdisciplinary work gets the chance to show its real value, but a more honest definition of “interdisciplinary”. In this time of knowledge abundance, interdisciplinary work can no longer get away with simply borrowing language from other fields. While cross-disciplinary name-dropping may suffice as a marketing strategy, true impact emerges from bringing people together and helping them see how their expertise is a necessary piece of a larger puzzle. 

Ego pushes us to prove that our approach is the best, or the only one needed. And as long as we expect a single approach to answer inherently complex questions, interdisciplinary remains an advertisement, not an empowered way of doing science.

However, I would argue that genuine interdisciplinary research is still at its rudimentary stages and craves to be nurtured and invested in by the wise who see its value. But do we actually have the infrastructure (cultural, institutional, and incentive-wise) for it? What are we missing, and what do we need to make it work?
