Perfectionism often makes taking action harder. We want to act responsibly, yet we fear acting imperfectly. Rarely is there a single correct choice or solution, though; merely trade-offs. Sometimes acting quickly feels like the right choice; other times, slowing down and acting more thoughtfully feels like the more responsible option.

Another layer of pressure comes from the idea that acting cautiously may be framed as fear, indifference, or even disloyalty; a heartbreaking oversimplification of reality. Yet caution, too, can come from care.

Some argue that one should not dwell on details, but instead act quickly to create momentum. However, I would argue that details do matter, especially in complex systems.

There is this concept of “chaotic systems” in dynamical systems theory that describes complex systems where tiny changes at the beginning can drastically alter their trajectory and how they evolve over time. What appears to be a small action can have outsized consequences later. And to me, real life often seems to resemble this kind of complexity. It seems reasonable, even responsible, then, to resist the urge to oversimplify what is fundamentally complex, especially when the stakes are high and long-term consequences are unclear. 

Being aware of this complexity, though, can make things feel heavier; creating anxiety, and potentially leading to inaction. But can inaction itself also be a legitimate response; a pause to observe and try to avoid unintended, long-lasting consequences?
