Humans have always had some level of specialization, with variation in the balance between depth and breadth. From generalists in early human societies built around versatility, to narrowly specialized individuals promising efficiency and progress. For a long time now, we’ve been breathing depth as the gold standard of excellence, treating the rest as dilution and a threat to high standards.

A familiar, classic model: training experts in increasingly narrow silos, then later bringing them together under the label of “collaboration”, with seemingly sound reasoning—highly specialized individuals, tightly coupled systems, maximum efficiency. Yet this approach often seems to reach diminishing returns and may rarely produce the kind of transformative progress we hope for. It can even prove detrimental—not just to growth and innovation, but to character and intellectual health.

Education and careers built around mastering a single train of thought make identity tightly bound to one’s niche. Reassuring within a closed ecosystem, yet fragile and deeply destabilizing once outside it. Validation becomes conditional. Curiosity shuts down as a form of self-preservation. Critique of ideas begins to feel like an attack on identity. Expertise becomes something to protect and gatekeep. Collaboration turns into competition over whose approach matters more, rather than how perspectives might complement one another.

At the same time, narrow specialization often under-delivers on its promise of progress, creating an illusion of progress, or at least a non-practical form of progress. A mathematically elegant theory of biology may feel complete and sophisticated while, in reality, missing biological meaning. Likewise, purely experimental work may overlook logical or theoretical inconsistencies. Fields advance internally, yet the larger questions remain unresolved.

There’s a growing sense that this emphasis on isolated niche expertise may no longer fit new realities. A call for a different rigor. One that reflects not a lowering of standards, but a shift in standards. One that becomes rejuvenating when growth within a niche reaches saturation and progress stagnates. Perhaps it is time for a re-emergence of cycles, where generalism regains value.

Or perhaps depth and breadth can coexist—time-consuming in nature, late-blooming in appearance, and often misread as aimless wandering.

Maybe wandering has always been part of discovery. Maybe our obsession with structure has been quietly constraining. And maybe wandering isn’t the absence of direction, but the early stage of finding one.
